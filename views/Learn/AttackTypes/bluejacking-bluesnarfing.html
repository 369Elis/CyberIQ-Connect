What Is Bluejacking?

Bluejacking is a relatively harmless Bluetooth exploit where attackers send unsolicited messages to nearby devices. It does not involve stealing personal data or taking control of the device. Instead, it is often used as a prank or marketing tactic to send texts, images, or sounds to unsuspecting users.

A hacker performing a Bluejacking attack first scans for active Bluetooth devices within a short range, typically around 30 feet. Once they find a device, they send an unsolicited message disguised as a standard Bluetooth notification. The victim may receive what appears to be a normal contact request or message, which can be confusing or annoying.

However, since no personal data is stolen, the primary risk is disruption and annoyance, rather than serious security threats.

What Is Bluesnarfing?

Bluesnarfing, in contrast, is a serious cybersecurity attack that allows hackers to steal sensitive data from a device through its Bluetooth connection. Unlike Bluejacking, which only sends messages, Bluesnarfing enables an attacker to access and copy personal files such as contact lists, text messages, emails, and even private photos.

The attack works by scanning for discoverable Bluetooth devices and exploiting security flaws in the protocol to bypass authentication. Once the attacker gains unauthorized access, they can extract private data without the victim noticing.

In some cases, advanced Bluesnarfing attacks even allow the hacker to send messages or make phone calls from the victimâ€™s device.
